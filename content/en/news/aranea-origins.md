I recently added *Aranea* to the script archive, but I wanted to provide a brief addendum regarding the thematic architecture of the project and how it came to be.

The initial impulse was to engage directly with comedy-horror. It is a highly entertaining genre that, at its best, possesses a profound tradition of social commentary. Much like *An American Werewolf in London* utilised a creature feature to dissect isolation and the stiff-upper-lip response to tragedy, *Aranea* uses its monster to dissect the absurdity of British upper-middle-class repression. 

To ground the narrative geometry, I leaned heavily into my own conceptualisation of the British countryside, populating the manor with characters drawn directly from memory. Crucially, the story is anchored by an autistic child. As a neurodivergent person, injecting my own sensory and observational experiences into the protagonist provided the personal stakes necessary to elevate the script beyond a simple genre exercise. 

The choice of a primeval spider as the central monstrous force was, at its core, born of a simple, personal fear. There is a visceral, crawling dread in the pervasive nature of an arachnid infecting a pristine, upmarket household. The creature acts as the physical manifestation of a deeper, underlying curse—the 'sins of the fathers'—that sets the tragedy in motion. However, the exact mechanics of this curse are kept intentionally ambiguous, allowing the audience space for their own interpretation.

Ultimately, *Aranea* represents my overarching aim as a screenwriter: to strike a precise balance between open-ended arthouse observation and the robust machinery of commercial genre cinema. The goal is always to keep an audience thought-provoked and guessing, whilst rigorously adhering to the cinematic tropes—the suspense, the comedy, the visceral horror, and the adventure—that bring us into the dark in the first place.
